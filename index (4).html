YouTube Search Experience Reimagined

When you search to buy something or learn something on YouTube, you get the same ranked list either way. These are two prototypes for what a smarter experience could look like, one for product discovery and one for informational queries, both powered by Gemini.

The two query types I focused on

1
Product discovery queries
"Best running shoes for beginners", "best laptop under $1000" , you know what you want to buy but YouTube gives you 40 review videos with no way to compare across them.
2
Informational + exploratory
"How to break into AI PM", "how to start investing" , you want a clear path but there's a whole landscape to understand first. YouTube gives you 50 videos and no sense of where to begin.

How I approached this
Step 1 , Finding the problem
Not all searches are the same, but YouTube treats them like they are

I started by thinking about the moments where YouTube search actually fails me. Navigational queries ("MrBeast latest video") work fine. But the moment I search for something I want to genuinely understand, the experience falls apart. A wall of videos with no sense of where to start, how they relate, or how deep I should go.

I mapped search queries across five intent types and zeroed in on two where the gap between user need and current experience is biggest: pure exploratory queries and hybrid informational-exploratory ones.

Step 2 , Defining the user need
Users doing research don't just want more videos. They want help deciding.

When someone searches "best running shoes for beginners," they're not looking for the most popular review. They're trying to make a decision, and every creator has a different top pick, different criteria, different budget. The job to be done is closer to "help me pick" than "show me something to watch."

The core insight: the problem isn't the quality of YouTube's content. It's that the interface gives you no way to compare across it when the query is about making a choice.

Step 3 , Designing the solution
Gemini can do the orientation work, if you let it

Gemini is already reading across YouTube's content at scale. The opportunity is to surface that understanding in the search UI: a topic map showing what the query breaks down into, a learning path that sequences videos by depth, and subtopic filters that let users self-direct.

I also decided early on that the solution had to be additive. The regular video grid stays exactly as it is. The new layer sits above it for users who want structure, and is easy to skip for those who don't.

Step 4 , Handling different intents differently
A product discovery query needs a different UI than an informational one

While prototyping, I noticed that "best running shoes for beginners" and "how to break into AI PM" feel similar on the surface but need different things. The first is about making a decision across competing options. The second is about understanding a landscape and finding a path through it.

So I built two distinct layouts. Product discovery queries lead with personalized picks, a synthesized highlights video, and a breakdown by topic. Informational queries lead with an action plan and a topic map. The intent detection happens in the background via Gemini, so the user just sees a result that fits what they were actually looking for.
