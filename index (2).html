YouTube Search Experience Reimagined

When you search for something you want to actually understand, YouTube gives you the same ranked list it always does. No structure, no starting point, no sense of the bigger picture. These are two prototypes for what a better experience could look like.

The two query types I focused on

1
Informational + exploratory
"How to break into AI PM", "how to start investing", "how to learn design" , you want a clear path, but there's a whole landscape to understand first. YouTube gives you 50 videos and no sense of where to begin.
2
Pure research queries
"What caused the 2008 financial crisis", "how does the brain work", "history of the internet" , you're not looking for one video. You want to actually understand a topic, but YouTube treats it like any other search.

How I approached this
Step 1 , Finding the problem
Not all searches are the same, but YouTube treats them like they are

I started by thinking about the moments where YouTube search actually fails me. Navigational queries ("MrBeast latest video") work fine. But the moment I search for something I want to genuinely understand, the experience falls apart. A wall of videos with no sense of where to start, how they relate, or how deep I should go.

I mapped search queries across five intent types and zeroed in on two where the gap between user need and current experience is biggest: pure exploratory queries and hybrid informational-exploratory ones.

Step 2 , Defining the user need
Users doing research don't just want more videos. They want orientation.

When someone searches "what caused the 2008 financial crisis," they're not looking for the single best video. They want to understand a topic with real depth and multiple angles. The job to be done is closer to "help me make sense of this" than "show me something to watch."

The core insight: the problem isn't the quality of YouTube's content. It's that the interface doesn't help users navigate it when the topic is complex.

Step 3 , Designing the solution
Gemini can do the orientation work, if you let it

Gemini is already reading across YouTube's content at scale. The opportunity is to surface that understanding in the search UI: a topic map showing what the query breaks down into, a learning path that sequences videos by depth, and subtopic filters that let users self-direct.

I also decided early on that the solution had to be additive. The regular video grid stays exactly as it is. The new layer sits above it for users who want structure, and is easy to skip for those who don't.

Step 4 , Handling different intents differently
A hybrid query needs a different UI than a pure research query

While prototyping, I noticed that "how to break into AI PM" and "what caused the 2008 financial crisis" feel similar on the surface but need different things. The first has a clear action orientation mixed with an exploratory one. The second is pure research with no obvious next step.

So I built two distinct layouts. Hybrid queries lead with a structured action plan. Pure exploratory queries lead with a topic map and learning path. The intent detection happens in the background via Gemini, so the user just sees a result that fits what they were actually looking for.
